## Abstract
I got [retinitis pigmentosa][0] since I was born, and I thought it would be a good idea to expose this upfront and talk about it, as it doesn't afflict a lot of people in the world, but still can raise eyebrows.

This document is:
* An attempt at describing this condition and what it implies on day to day life and work.
* A way, I hope, to encourage people suffering from similar conditions to persevere and show them it's possible to pursue a successful professional career.

However, it is NOT:
* An article from an expert on the topic. I'll do my best to describe symptoms and conditions, and I'll add sources when appropriate.
* A call for pity. If we work together, I'd rather have you fully aware than wondering.
* A defense or an argument for [positive discrimination][1]. I think this is an awful solution to an awful issue that only produces jealousy, neurosis and bitterness, and I'd rather have you hire me for my skills rather than to satisfy your quotas.

### My time at school was fun
Not being able to read at the board at all, not being able to read on "toilet paper"-sized photocopies and not being able to read many books (especially when sharing with my neighbor) was a huge issue at school.

So I kept listening.

It didn't work well for all science-related disciplines though, and the struggle was so intense I wasn't really keen to compensate with my friends' notes either. I bet many teachers weren't aware of my situation though and thought I was just a bad student looking at the clock (which I wouldn't see anyway).

The only real exceptions were computer science related disciplines, because I was already the "nerd who knows how to tick a checkbox in MSN Messenger" and I could teach hexadecimal better than the teacher himself at 12. And I wasn't too bad in French, English, philosophy, economy or history.

For parents having childrens in my situation though, I would strongly suggest finding a school specialized on blindness and low vision.

### Working on a computer got better
Accessibility tools got better over time I think, at least the ones installed by default on most modern operating systems, which take advantage of the GPU for interface rendering, allowing for various advanced and useful effects.

One of the most precious tools is the universal magnifier, set up on a special "full screen" mode which zooms in the entire screen and follow the mouse cursor (or, optionally, keyboard focus). Both OS X and Windows 7+ have very decent magnifiers, and some Linux desktop environments too, provided you're lucky with graphics drivers.

Dark themes and high contrast modes have been available for a while, but I think the advent of night modes is one of the biggest recent improvement for both healthcare and accessibility.

Also, the web changed, shifting from being primarily targeted on Windows users on almost fixed resolutions, to targetting a wide range of devices with various resolutions, forcing designers to rethink their layout. This also result in a better support for browsers zoom feature: instead of displaying a mishapen website, elements start collapsing, eventually switching to their mobile version :-)

Browsers evolved too. Firefox was one of the first to provide a decent zoom feature (which would increase both text and images correctly) and that feature kept getting improvements. Flash was killed, and Scalable Vector Graphics were introduced to handle those resolutions changes nicely. Bye-bye blurry pictures.

Many applications, whether they rely or not on web technologies, also provide their own zoom-in or accessibility features.

Using a voice-over assistant is also an option, though I haven't explored this too much. I have seen only a few developers using it, and I'm sure you can become very, very productive with it.

In my particular case, I'd say my vision slows me a bit; it makes reading long digital articles tedious, and reading books impossible. Fortunately, I'm not alone for the former, as `TL;DR` is a well-spread meme, and the latter is solved by digital versions, especially ebooks.

I also have to double check my work, which makes me cautious, or unsure to many eyes. I tend to insert doubt when I am sure of a thing, even though I triple checked that thing already, and it can be an issue when arguing or asserting myself.

### Give me a torch and sunglasses
I got almost no night vision. Thankfully, we all got a flashlight on our smartphones now, but it can still be a bit dangerous to walk at night in an uncivilized city like Paris.

I am also extra sensitive to light and can be easily blinded by artificial or natural light (and I don't look at the sun that quite often, I swear).

### Compensating with superpowers
Since I have been relying on hearing and memorization, I have developed those two aspects quite a lot, at least when my smartphone can't do the job, like giving me the list of train stations or train hours, or the menu of a restaurant in advance. Some menus are a bit hard to read, so I tend to memorize one or two things when asking people feels too awkward or unconvenient.

I also got a good hearing and rely a lot on it for social interaction, since people faces are blurry most of the time, and despite the fact that I endangered my eardrums with lowcost earbuds and dubious music in high school, I am still performing on the high end of the spectrum.
