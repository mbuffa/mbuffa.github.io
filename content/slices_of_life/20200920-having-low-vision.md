## Abstract
I got [retinitis pigmentosa][0] since I was born, and I thought it would be a good idea to expose this upfront and talk about it, as it is a bit uncommon.

This document is:
* An attempt at describing this condition and what it implies on day to day life and work.
* A way to formalize hypotheses on topics outside of office work.
* A way, I hope, to encourage people suffering from similar conditions to persevere and show them it's possible to pursue a successful professional career, and thrive.

However, it is NOT:
* An article from an expert on the topic. I'll do my best to describe symptoms and conditions, and I'll add sources when appropriate.
* A call for pity. If we work together, I'd rather have you fully aware than wondering and raising your eyebrows ;)
* A defense or an argument in favor of [positive discrimination][1]. I think this is an awful solution that only produces jealousy, neurosis and bitterness, and I'd rather have you hire me for my skills rather than to satisfy your quotas to pay less taxes.

But I'd like to continue by covering specific topics.

### Night vision and light
I got almost no night vision. Thankfully, we all got a flashlight on our smartphones now, but it can still be a bit dangerous to walk at night in an uncivilized city like Paris. I also got to watch my step and be concentrated to avoid bumping into someone.

I am also sensitive to light and can be easily blinded by artificial or natural light (and I don't look at the sun that quite often, I swear).

### School was fun
Not being able to read at the board at all, not being able to read on "toilet paper"-sized photocopies and not being able to read many books (especially when sharing one in class) was a huge issue at school. So I kept listening.

It didn't work well for many scientific disciplines though, and the struggle was real. To be honest, I was also more interested in things I was already good at, or at least in which I wasn't overwhelmed by my disability. I did great in computer science classes though, because I was the nerdy boy and I could teach hexadecimal better than teachers at 12. Also, the level was pretty low :)

### Working on a computer got better
Accessibility tools got better over time I think, at least the ones installed by default on most modern operating systems, which take advantage of the GPU for interface rendering, allowing for various advanced and useful effects.

One of the most precious tools is the universal magnifier, set up on a special "full screen" mode which zooms in the entire screen and follow the mouse cursor (or, optionally, keyboard focus). Both OS X and Windows 7+ have very decent magnifiers, and some Linux desktop environments too, provided you're lucky with graphics drivers.

Dark themes and high contrast modes have been available for a while, but I think the advent of night modes is one of the biggest recent improvements for both healthcare and accessibility.

Also, the web changed, shifting from being primarily targeted at Windows users on almost fixed resolutions, to targetting a wide range of devices with various resolutions, forcing designers to rethink their layout. This also result in a better support for browsers zoom feature: instead of displaying a mishapen website, elements start collapsing, eventually switching to their mobile version :-)

Browsers evolved too. Firefox was one of the first to provide a decent zoom feature (which would increase both text and images correctly) and that feature kept being improved. Flash was killed, and Scalable Vector Graphics were introduced to handle those resolutions changes nicely. Bye-bye blurry pictures.

Many applications, whether they rely or not on web technologies, also provide their own zoom-in or accessibility features.

Using a voice-over assistant is also an option, though I haven't explored this too much. I have seen only a few developers using it, and I'm sure you can become very, very productive with it.

In my particular case, I'd say my vision slows me a bit; it makes reading long digital articles tedious, and reading books impossible. Fortunately, there are ebooks.

I also have to double check my work, which makes me cautious, or unsure to many eyes. I tend to insert doubt when I am sure of a thing, even though I triple checked that thing already, and it can be an issue when it comes to arguing, asserting, and confirming.

### Impact on day-to-day life
Having such a low vision basically rules out driving most kind of vehicles, including bicycles, at least in crowded cities where it's already a bit dangerous with healthy eyes.

Public transportation is my way to go, and even in some scenarios, a smartphone is still a welcome assistant, mostly for maps.

Any sports that relies on reactivity, precision and a truly stereoscopic vision is also ruled out, but there's still a wide range of activities that can be picked.

Fortunately, hobbies aren't so impacted. Even when good vision is required, you can still thrive with determination, efforts and creativity.

However, in my case, visiting a museum isn't very interesting without a guide, and even though I can go to a movie theater, I feel I'm not building the same "emotional connection" with the actors as I do when watching a movie on a screen a few centimeters from my nose. It certainly has to do with the fact that I don't get the small details on people's faces, and I think I like music and sound better than the other forms of arts for the same reason. It probably impacts social life as well, as it's easier for me to feel emotions from a voice rather than a face.

### Compensating with superpowers
Since I have been relying on hearing and memorising, I have developed those two aspects quite a lot, at least when my smartphone is out of battery.

A smartphone can't always give me the list of train stations or train hours, or the menu of a restaurant in advance. Some menus are a bit hard to read, so I tend to memorize one or two things when asking people feels too awkward or unpleasant.

I also got a good hearing and rely a lot on it for social interaction, since people faces are blurry most of the time, and despite the fact that I endangered my eardrums with lowcost earbuds and dubious music in high school, I was still at the very top at my last hearing test.

I also got a good sense of direction in general.
